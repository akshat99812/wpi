Executive Summary

Building a WAsP-class micrositing & EYA engine

Micrositing is the discipline of deciding exactly where each wind turbine stands on a site so that the array captures the most energy at the lowest cost and risk, while respecting terrain, wakes, loads, noise, and environmental constraints. Energy Yield Assessment (EYA) is the quantitative engine behind every wind-farm investment decision: it converts a wind measurement and a turbine layout into a bankable estimate of long-term production, expressed as exceedance probabilities (P50, P90) with a defensible uncertainty budget.

This document is a build-oriented specification for a software platform that performs both tasks at the standard set by WAsP (DTU), WindPRO (EMD), openWind (UL Solutions) and WindFarmer (DNV) — and extends them with the modelling techniques that have matured in 2023–2026: CFD and mesoscale–microscale coupling for complex terrain, and machine-learning surrogates and super-resolution downscaling for speed and accuracy. The scope is onshore wind, where terrain, roughness and orographic flow dominate — WAsP's home domain and the typical setting for Indian wind projects.

What the platform must do — eight factor domains

A credible micrositing/EYA tool is an integration of eight modelling domains, each a chapter in this blueprint: (1) the wind climate — measurement, long-term correction and the statistical wind resource; (2) terrain, roughness and obstacles; (3) the flow model that extrapolates the measured climate across the site and to hub height; (4) wake interaction between turbines; (5) the power conversion and full loss waterfall that turns wind into net energy; (6) uncertainty quantification and P-value reporting; (7) site suitability and turbine-load validation against IEC 61400-1; and (8) layout optimisation under physical, regulatory and environmental constraints.

The modelling spectrum we recommend

No single flow model is correct everywhere. The platform is architected around a pluggable flow-engine interface so that the same site, measurements and turbines can be run through four engines of increasing fidelity and cost: a fast linearised model (WAsP IBZ-class) for flat-to-moderate terrain; RANS/LES CFD for steep, separated terrain where linear theory fails; mesoscale–microscale coupling (WRF → microscale, the New European Wind Atlas / Global Wind Atlas 4 methodology) for large or data-sparse sites; and ML surrogates that emulate CFD/mesoscale fields at a fraction of the run-time. A ruggedness diagnostic (RIX/ΔRIX) automatically flags where the linear engine is out of validity and a higher-fidelity engine should take over.

Design thesisThe differentiator is not any one model but the integration: one data model, one uncertainty framework, and one optimisation loop wrapped around interchangeable flow and wake engines, with standards-compliant reporting (IEC 61400-15-1:2025, and the IEC 61400-15-2 energy-yield digital exchange format) generated automatically.

How to build it

Part IV translates the science into software: a layered architecture (data ingestion → terrain/roughness preprocessing → flow engine → wake engine → energy & loss → uncertainty → optimisation → GIS visualisation → reporting), a recommended open-source foundation (PyWake, TopFarm, FLORIS, WindNinja, OpenFOAM/WRF, the geospatial Python stack), a concrete data-source catalogue (ERA5, Global Wind Atlas 4, Copernicus DEM, ESA WorldCover, and India's NIWE atlas), and a five-phase roadmap that delivers a usable linear-model EYA tool in Phase 1 and adds wakes, optimisation, CFD/mesoscale and ML in later phases.

The remainder of this document develops each of these elements in full, with the governing equations, typical parameter values, default loss and uncertainty tables, validation strategy, and a competitive comparison against the incumbent commercial tools.

Part I — Foundations & Methodology

1. Introduction, Scope & What "WAsP-class" Means

1.1 The problem in one paragraph

Given a parcel of land, a few months to a few years of wind measurements from one or more masts or remote-sensing devices, a digital description of the terrain, and a catalogue of candidate turbines, two questions must be answered with money on the line: where do we put the turbines (micrositing) and how much energy will they produce over 20+ years, and how sure are we (energy yield assessment). Every number that follows in a project's financial model — debt sizing, tariff bids, equity returns — descends from the P50 and P90 energy figures this tool produces. The engineering challenge is that the wind at a turbine 800 m away and 40 m higher than the mast is not the wind at the mast: it has been reshaped by terrain, roughness, the atmospheric boundary layer, and the wakes of neighbouring turbines. Modelling that transformation accurately, and bounding its error honestly, is the whole game.

1.2 What "WAsP-class" means as an engineering target

WAsP (Wind Atlas Analysis and Application Program, DTU Wind Energy; current release WAsP 12.10, December 2025) defined the industry's reference workflow: take an observed wind climate, generalise it upward and outward by removing the local effects of terrain, roughness and obstacles to obtain a site-independent regional (generalised) wind climate, then predict the wind climate at any other point by re-applying the local effects there. To match and exceed WAsP, our platform must reproduce that round-trip with at least equal fidelity and add the things WAsP does not natively do well — high-fidelity flow in steep terrain, integrated multi-turbine layout optimisation, automated uncertainty, and modern data-driven acceleration.

"WAsP-class" — the acceptance barThe tool is WAsP-class when, on a benchmark onshore site with a validation mast withheld, it predicts the withheld mast's mean wind speed within ±2–3% in simple terrain, flags (via RIX/ΔRIX) where it cannot, produces a full P50/P90 EYA with a documented uncertainty budget, and optimises a turbine layout under realistic constraints — all from one project data model and reproducibly.

1.3 Scope of this blueprint

  • In scope: onshore wind; the full measurement-to-yield chain; four flow engines (linear, CFD, mesoscale, ML); analytical wake and blockage modelling; gross-to-net loss accounting; uncertainty and P-values; IEC 61400-1 site-suitability checks; layout optimisation; and the software architecture, data pipeline, technology stack and roadmap to build it.
  • Adjacent / lighter touch: offshore array effects and met-ocean modelling (the architecture supports them but they are not the focus); detailed aeroelastic load simulation (we validate suitability and hand off to dedicated load tools such as OpenFAST); grid and electrical design beyond the loss budget.

2. The Physics of the Wind Resource

The flow engine is only as good as the boundary-layer physics it encodes. This chapter sets out the equations the tool must implement to describe how wind speed varies with height, terrain and time, and how that variability becomes an energy-bearing statistical distribution.

2.1 The atmospheric boundary layer (ABL)

Wind turbines live in the lowest few hundred metres of the atmosphere — the atmospheric boundary layer, where the ground exerts friction and surface heating/cooling drives turbulence. Within the lowest ~10% (the surface layer), momentum flux is approximately constant with height and the mean wind profile follows the logarithmic law. Above it, in the Ekman layer, the Coriolis force turns the wind with height until it reaches the geostrophic value aloft. A micrositing tool models the surface and lower-Ekman layers explicitly and uses the geostrophic wind as the upper anchor for the wind-atlas generalisation.

2.2 The logarithmic wind profile and roughness length

For neutral stratification the mean horizontal wind speed grows logarithmically with height above a surface characterised by an aerodynamic roughness length z0:

U(z) = (u* / κ) · ln(z / z0)Eq. 2.1 — log law (neutral)

where u* is the friction velocity, κ ≈ 0.40 is the von Kármán constant, and z0 ranges from ~0.0002 m (water) and 0.03 m (open farmland) to 0.4–1.0 m (forest, suburb). Non-neutral stability adds a Monin–Obukhov correction ψm that is a function of the stability parameter z/L (L = Obukhov length):

U(z) = (u* / κ) · [ ln(z / z0) − ψm(z / L) ]Eq. 2.2 — with stability

L>0 is stable (nocturnal, suppressed mixing, strong shear), L<0 unstable (daytime convection, well-mixed, low shear), L→∞ neutral. The tool must at minimum support a neutral profile and ideally a stability-classified climatology, because shear directly sets the hub-height wind and the rotor-equivalent wind speed.

2.3 The power law (engineering shear)

For day-to-day extrapolation the power law is ubiquitous because it needs only a single fitted exponent α:

U(z) / U(zr) = ( z / zr )αEq. 2.3 — power law

Typical α: ~0.10–0.14 over open, flat terrain; 0.20–0.30 over forest or in stable nocturnal conditions; occasionally >0.3. α is best derived from two or more measurement heights rather than assumed. Caution: extrapolating a single-height measurement to hub height with an assumed α is a leading source of EYA error and must carry an explicit uncertainty (Chapter 12).

2.4 Geostrophic wind & the resistance (drag) law

The wind-atlas method connects the surface friction velocity to the free-atmosphere geostrophic wind G through the geostrophic drag law, the theoretical backbone of upward generalisation:

G = (u* / κ) · √{ [ ln(u* / (f z0)) − A ]² + B² }Eq. 2.4 — resistance law

with Coriolis parameter f = 2Ω sin φ (φ = latitude, Ω = Earth's rotation rate) and empirical constants A≈1.8, B≈4.5 for neutral conditions. Because G is (to first order) independent of local surface conditions, two nearby sites with different roughness share the same geostrophic forcing — this is precisely what lets WAsP generalise a mast's climate "up" and re-apply it "down" at a turbine location.

2.5 The Weibull distribution & wind power density

The long-term distribution of 10-minute mean wind speeds at a height is described, per direction sector, by a two-parameter Weibull distribution:

f(U) = (k / A) · (U / A)k−1 · exp[ −(U / A)k ]Eq. 2.5 — Weibull pdf

where A (scale, m/s) is proportional to the mean speed and k (shape, dimensionless, typically 1.8–2.6 onshore) describes the spread. The mean speed and the all-important mean of U³ (which carries the energy) follow from the Gamma function Γ:

Ū = A · Γ(1 + 1/k)   ;    <U³> = A³ · Γ(1 + 3/k)Eq. 2.6 — Weibull moments

The instantaneous power available in the wind through a swept area Ar is the cube law that makes accurate wind-speed prediction so valuable — a 1% error in speed is roughly a 3% error in energy:

P = ½ · ρ · Ar · U³   ⟹    WPD = ½ · ρ · A³ · Γ(1 + 3/k)Eq. 2.7 — wind power density
Why ±1% speed mattersBecause P ∝ U³, the platform's flow-model accuracy target (±2–3% in speed) translates to roughly ±6–9% in gross energy before wakes and losses. This single fact justifies every chapter that follows on flow modelling and uncertainty.

2.6 Air density

Power scales linearly with air density ρ, which varies with temperature, pressure, altitude and humidity. The tool must normalise power curves to site density (IEC 61400-12-1). For moist air:

ρ = pd / (Rd T) + pv / (Rv T)Eq. 2.8 — moist-air density

with Rd=287.06, Rv=461.5 J·kg⁻¹·K⁻¹; reference ρ=1.225 kg·m⁻³ at 15 °C, sea level. A site at 1,000 m and 25 °C may sit near 1.06 kg·m⁻³ — a ~13% power penalty that must not be overlooked for elevated Indian plateau sites (e.g., parts of Maharashtra, Karnataka, Rajasthan).

2.7 Turbulence intensity

Turbulence intensity TI = σU/Ū (ratio of the 10-min standard deviation of wind speed to its mean) governs both energy (it broadens the effective power curve) and, critically, fatigue loads and turbine-class suitability (Chapter 13). Ambient TI falls with height and wind speed and rises with surface roughness; wakes add to it (Chapter 10). The tool stores TI per sector and wind-speed bin, not as a single number.

3.2 Campaign design the tool should encode

  • Duration: ≥12 continuous months to resolve the seasonal cycle; the long-term correction (Chapter 4) then extends this to the climatological norm.
  • Heights: ≥3 measurement levels to fit shear directly rather than assume it; top level as close to hub height as practical.
  • Coverage: enough masts/RSDs to characterise spatial variability across complex terrain; one mast cannot represent a ridge system.
  • Data availability: target ≥90% recovery after QC; document gaps and their treatment.

3.3 Quality control & processing (a required module)

The ingestion layer must implement an auditable QC chain: range and step checks; flatline/stuck-sensor detection; icing flags (low σ with sub-zero temperature); tower-shadow and boom-distortion sector exclusion for masts; CNR/availability filtering for LiDAR; inter-sensor consistency; and gap treatment. Each step is logged so the data lineage is reproducible — a lender requirement. Output of this stage is a clean, height-resolved time series plus a per-sensor uncertainty estimate.

3.4 From time series to wind climate

The cleaned series is reduced to the statistical objects the flow engine consumes: a directional wind rose (frequency by sector, usually 12 sectors of 30°), a sector-wise Weibull (A, k per sector), the shear and veer profiles, the TI by sector and speed bin, and the air-density climatology. In WAsP terms this is the observed wind climate (OWC) — the starting point for generalisation in Chapter 6.

Software implicationRepresent the wind climate as a first-class, serialisable object (sectors × {A, k, frequency, TI, shear, ρ}) with provenance. This object — not the raw time series — is what the flow engine, wake engine and reporting all consume, mirroring WAsP's .tab/.owc and .lib structures so external interoperability is trivial.

4. Long-Term Correction & the Reference Climate (MCP)

A 12–24 month campaign captures a sample, not the climate. The wind varies year to year by several percent, so the measured period must be corrected to a long-term reference before it can underpin a 20-year financial model. This is Measure–Correlate–Predict (MCP).

4.1 The principle

MCP correlates the short on-site measurement against a long, continuous reference dataset (a nearby long-running mast or, far more commonly today, a reanalysis time series), builds a transfer relationship over the concurrent period, and applies it to the reference's full multi-decade history to synthesise what the site would have experienced long-term. The corrected long-term wind climate is then the input to all flow modelling.

4.2 MCP methods the tool should offer

  • Linear regression (variance-ratio / total least squares), per direction sector — the workhorse; preserves the variance of the predicted series.
  • Matrix / joint-distribution methods that map the full speed-direction distribution rather than the mean, capturing non-linearity.
  • Weibull-scale MCP operating on distribution parameters per sector.
  • Machine-learning MCP (random forests, gradient boosting, neural nets) that ingest multiple reference predictors and capture non-linear, stability-dependent relationships — increasingly used 2023–2026 and worth supporting as an advanced option (Chapter 9).

4.4 Representativeness, inter-annual variability & the uncertainty it injects

Two distinct uncertainties enter here and must be tracked separately into Chapter 12. Long-term consistency: how well the reference reproduces the true long-term trend and the concurrent correlation (poor correlation → large MCP uncertainty, typically 1–4% on the long-term mean). Inter-annual variability (IAV): the year-to-year scatter of the wind itself, typically a standard deviation of ~4–6% of the mean annual energy onshore. IAV is what makes a single future year uncertain even with a perfect long-term mean — and it is the term that shrinks with the project's assessment horizon (it divides by √N over N years), which is why P90 over 10 years is higher than P90 over 1 year (Chapter 12).

Build noteMCP and IAV are not optional niceties — they are the difference between a "wind speed" and a bankable wind speed. Ship MCP as a core module with at least linear and matrix methods, automatic concurrent-period detection, sector handling, and an uncertainty output that feeds the P-value engine directly.

Part II — The Modelling Engines

5. Terrain, Roughness & Obstacle Modelling

The flow engine reshapes the wind climate using three descriptions of the ground: elevation (orography), roughness (surface friction), and obstacles (local shelter). Building these layers correctly, at the right resolution, is half of micrositing accuracy.

5.1 Elevation — digital elevation models

Orography drives speed-up over hills and ridges and flow separation behind steep slopes. The tool ingests a DEM, fits a high-resolution grid around each calculation point, and (for the linear model) builds the zooming polar grid of Chapter 6. Recommended sources, in order of preference where available:

5.2 Roughness — land cover to z₀

The tool converts a land-cover raster into a roughness-length map by a lookup table (Table 2.1), then into the roughness rose each calculation point sees upwind. Recommended land-cover sources: ESA WorldCover (10 m, global), Copernicus Global Land Cover (100 m), CORINE (Europe, 100 m), and OpenStreetMap for fine features (settlements, tree lines). Roughness changes upwind generate internal boundary layers that the flow model must grow downwind:

U(z) after a roughness change: blended between upstream and downstream log profiles below the IBL height h(x) ∝ x0.8Eq. 5.1 — internal boundary layer

Practically, the tool implements WAsP's roughness-change model: each upwind roughness transition modifies the local profile, and the net effect is integrated over the roughness rose. Editable roughness (the analyst draws/erases roughness lines and areas over imagery) is a must-have UI capability, mirroring WAsP's roughness-map editor.

5.3 Forests & displacement height

Tall vegetation does not just raise z₀ — it lifts the whole profile by a displacement height d (≈0.6–0.8 × canopy height), so the log law becomes U(z) = (u*/κ)·ln((z−d)/z₀). Ignoring d over forest can overstate hub-height shear and energy. For forested sites the tool should support a forest layer (height + density) feeding either a displacement-height correction (linear model) or an explicit canopy-drag term (CFD, Chapter 7).

5.4 Obstacles & shelter

Near-field obstacles (buildings, shelterbelts) cast wakes described by porosity and dimensions; the tool reduces wind speed within the sheltered zone (Perera/WAsP obstacle model) for any calculation point inside an obstacle's influence. Obstacles matter mostly for low masts and small/distributed turbines; for utility hub heights they are usually secondary but must still be representable.

5.5 Ruggedness — RIX and ΔRIX (the validity gate)

Linearised flow models assume attached flow; they break down when slopes exceed a critical value and the flow separates. The Ruggedness Index (RIX) quantifies this as the percentage of terrain within a radius around a point that is steeper than a critical slope (default 0.3, i.e. ~17°):

RIX(point) = % of upwind terrain with |∇ elevation| > 0.3Eq. 5.2 — ruggedness index

The decisive diagnostic is ΔRIX = RIX(prediction site) − RIX(measurement site). When a mast and a turbine sit in terrain of different ruggedness, a linear model's speed-up error correlates strongly with ΔRIX — and can be corrected for, or used as a trigger to escalate to CFD. The platform computes RIX/ΔRIX automatically for every mast–turbine pair and surfaces it as the flow-engine selection signal.

Automation the incumbents lackUse ΔRIX as a hard gate in the pipeline: |ΔRIX| below a threshold (say a few %) → the fast linear engine is trustworthy; above it → flag the prediction, apply a ΔRIX correction, and/or route that sub-domain to the CFD engine. This turns a known WAsP limitation into an automated, auditable decision.

6. Flow Model I — Linearised (WAsP-class IBZ / BZ)

The linear engine is the fast, robust default that made WAsP an industry standard. It is exact enough for flat-to-moderate terrain, runs in seconds, and provides the wind-atlas generalisation that underpins interoperability. The platform implements it first (Phase 1).

6.1 The wind-atlas method: generalise then predict

The central idea (Figure 4): an observed wind climate is contaminated by the local terrain, roughness and obstacles at the mast. Generalisation strips these effects away — extrapolating up through the orographic and roughness perturbations and the IBL — to yield a generalised (regional) wind climate referenced to standard roughness classes and several standard heights, valid over a region. Prediction reverses the process at a turbine location: re-apply that point's terrain, roughness and obstacle effects to the generalised climate to obtain its predicted wind climate, Weibull A/k and energy.

6.2 The three sub-models

  • Roughness-change model (§5.2): adjusts the profile for upwind roughness transitions via internal boundary layers, integrated over the roughness rose.
  • Obstacle (shelter) model (§5.4): reduces speed in the lee of porous obstacles.
  • Orographic model — the BZ model: the heart of the linear engine. It solves the linearised (Jackson–Hunt) equations for potential-flow-like perturbations over terrain on a high-resolution "zooming" polar grid (BZ = Bessel expansion on a Zooming grid) centred on each calculation point — fine near the point, coarse far away — capturing fractional speed-up over hills and slow-down in valleys.

6.3 Jackson–Hunt speed-up

For a low, smooth hill of height H and characteristic length L (half-width at half-height), linear theory predicts a fractional speed-up near the summit at low heights:

ΔS = ΔU / U ≈ 2 · (H / L) · (decay with height z / l)Eq. 6.1 — Jackson–Hunt speed-up

where the perturbation decays above an inner-layer scale l. The key qualitative results the engine reproduces: speed-up scales with hill slope H/L, peaks near the summit and a little above the surface, and decays with height — the physical basis for siting turbines on smooth ridge crests.

6.4 Vertical & horizontal extrapolation

Vertically, the engine combines the log law (Eq. 2.1–2.2), the geostrophic drag law (Eq. 2.4) and the roughness/IBL model to move the climate between heights. Horizontally, it applies the orographic and roughness perturbations at the target location. The output per calculation point is a full predicted wind climate: sector-wise Weibull A/k, mean speed, power density, and (gridded over the site) the Wind Resource Grid (WRG) consumed by the energy and optimisation modules.

6.5 Strengths, limits, and the ΔRIX correction

The linear model is fast, transparent, well-validated, and excellent in simple terrain — but, being linearised, it cannot represent flow separation and systematically errs in steep terrain. WAsP's own guidance: trust it when |ΔRIX| between mast and prediction site is small; apply a ΔRIX-based correction or escalate to CFD otherwise.

Ucorrected ≈ Ulinear · ( 1 + αRIX · ΔRIX )Eq. 6.2 — empirical ΔRIX correction

with a site/terrain-dependent coefficient αRIX calibrated from validation data. The platform automates this: compute ΔRIX (§5.5), apply the correction, and record both raw and corrected predictions with the associated uncertainty.

Phase-1 deliverableA working linear engine — wind-atlas generalisation, the three sub-models, WRG output, and ΔRIX gating — is the minimum viable flow core. With Chapters 3–4 (climate) and 11 (energy) it already constitutes a usable EYA tool, before any CFD, mesoscale or ML is added.

7. Flow Model II — CFD (RANS & LES)

Where linear theory fails — steep ridges, escarpments, gorges, forest edges, recirculation zones — computational fluid dynamics resolves the non-linear flow by solving the Navier–Stokes equations on a 3-D mesh of the terrain. The platform offers RANS as the workhorse and LES as the high-fidelity reference.

7.1 RANS — Reynolds-Averaged Navier–Stokes

RANS solves for the mean flow, modelling all turbulence through a closure. The standard onshore choice is the two-equation k–ε model (turbulent kinetic energy k and dissipation ε), often in an ABL-modified form, with k–ω SST preferred where separation and adverse pressure gradients matter (it behaves better near walls and in detachment). The eddy-viscosity closure is:

νt = Cμ · k² / ε   (standard Cμ ≈ 0.09)Eq. 7.1 — eddy viscosity

A correct, horizontally-homogeneous ABL must be maintained across the domain before terrain is introduced; this requires the Richards–Hoxey inlet profiles and wall treatment and a modified set of k–ε constants consistent with the log law:

k = u*² / √Cμ  ;   ε(z) = u*³ / ( κ (z + z0) )Eq. 7.2 — Richards–Hoxey ABL

Forests enter RANS as a canopy drag sink term in the momentum and turbulence equations (proportional to leaf-area density and U²), not merely as elevated roughness — important for sites in or near plantations.

7.2 LES — Large-Eddy Simulation

LES resolves the large, energy-containing turbulent eddies directly and models only the sub-grid scales (e.g. Smagorinsky or dynamic closures). It captures unsteady separation, wake meandering and turbulence statistics that RANS cannot — at 10²–10⁴× the cost. In the platform, LES is reserved for: (i) reference/validation runs on a few critical positions, (ii) generating training data for ML surrogates (Chapter 9), and (iii) detailed wake studies. Turbines in LES/RANS are represented by actuator-disk (momentum sink over the rotor) or actuator-line models rather than resolved blades.

7.3 Meshing & workflow

The CFD module must automate the painful parts: build a terrain-conforming mesh from the DEM (refined near the surface and around turbines, y⁺ control at the wall), map roughness/forest onto patches, run one solve per direction sector (typically 12), and assemble the sector results — weighted by the wind-climate sector frequencies — into the same WRG object the linear engine produces. This "results-compatible" design means everything downstream (energy, wakes, optimisation) is engine-agnostic.

7.4 Build vs integrate

Writing a CFD solver is out of scope; the platform orchestrates an existing one. Recommended: an OpenFOAM-based pipeline (open-source, scriptable, with established ABL and actuator-disk capability) wrapped by the platform's meshing/run/post-processing automation, with optional connectors to commercial engines (WAsP CFD, Meteodyn WT) for users who own them. Lattice-Boltzmann GPU solvers (e.g. ZephyCloud-class) are an attractive fast-CFD option to watch.

8. Flow Model III — Mesoscale & Coupling

Mesoscale numerical weather prediction supplies the regional wind climate where no long measurement exists, captures phenomena linear/CFD microscale models miss (thermal flows, channelling, large-scale gradients), and is the engine behind the public wind atlases the tool consumes.

8.1 WRF and the mesoscale climate

The Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) model integrates the atmosphere at ~1–10 km over months to decades, producing a physically-consistent, multi-year wind time series at the site — independent of, or complementary to, reanalysis. Its limitation is resolution: at 3 km it cannot see the individual ridge a turbine stands on. That gap is closed by coupling to a microscale model.

8.2 Mesoscale–microscale coupling & the wind-atlas methodology

The modern resource-assessment paradigm (used by NEWA and the Global Wind Atlas) is a two-step chain: run WRF to build a mesoscale climatology, then generalise and downscale it through a microscale model (WAsP-class linear, or CFD) to turbine-relevant resolution. The microscale step removes the mesoscale model's own coarse terrain/roughness and re-applies fine-resolution terrain/roughness — exactly the generalisation round-trip of Chapter 6, but starting from a modelled rather than a measured climate.

  • New European Wind Atlas (NEWA): WRF at 3 km coupled to microscale, delivered at ~30 m over 10 turbine-relevant heights, with a public mesoscale time-series API — the reference example of the method.
  • Global Wind Atlas 4 (released June 2025; auto-imported by WAsP 12.10): global microscale generalisation (~250 m) of mesoscale data — the tool's recommended first-look resource and spatial prior.
  • India — NIWE: the National Institute of Wind Energy publishes meso–micro-coupled potential maps at 100 m, 120 m and 150 m AGL at 500 m spatial resolution, validated against 400+ measurement masts (gross potential ~302 GW at 100 m, ~695 GW at 120 m, ~1,164 GW at 150 m). The tool should connect to the NIWE resource portal for Indian projects.

8.3 When the platform uses mesoscale

  • Prospecting/screening before any mast exists (use GWA4 / NIWE / NEWA).
  • As the MCP long-term reference at sites where reanalysis correlates poorly (complex terrain, monsoon-driven regimes).
  • To extend short campaigns and to characterise inter-annual variability over decades.
  • As the upper boundary condition / forcing for microscale CFD on large sites.
Integration, not reinventionThe platform need not run WRF in-house initially — it should consume NEWA/GWA4/NIWE products and reanalysis via their APIs, with an optional WRF-run capability added later (Phase 4) for clients needing bespoke mesoscale time series.

9. Flow Model IV — ML / AI-Augmented Modelling 2023–2026

The newest engine class does not replace physics — it accelerates and sharpens it. Machine learning now delivers CFD-quality flow fields in milliseconds, super-resolves coarse mesoscale winds to turbine scale, and improves long-term correction. This is the "latest technology" layer that differentiates a 2026-era tool from a 2010-era one.

9.1 Super-resolution downscaling

Borrowing image super-resolution architectures (CNNs, GANs and — increasingly — diffusion models and transformers), these models learn the mapping from coarse wind fields (e.g. ERA5 ~31 km, or WRF 3 km) plus high-resolution terrain to fine-scale winds, validated against LiDAR/mast or high-resolution reanalysis (e.g. COSMO-REA). Reported results (2024–2025) show skilful downscaling from ~9 km to ~1 km and below. In the platform, a trained downscaler turns a public mesoscale product into a site-resolution prior in seconds — a fast alternative to a full CFD pass for screening.

9.2 CFD surrogates / emulators

A surrogate is trained on a corpus of CFD/LES solutions (parameterised by terrain, roughness, inflow direction and stability) to predict the flow field for a new case without solving the PDEs. Graph- and convolution-based neural operators and physics-informed neural networks (PINNs, which embed the governing equations in the loss) reproduce RANS-class accuracy at a fraction of the run-time, enabling CFD-quality fields inside an optimisation loop that would otherwise be intractable. Multi-fidelity transfer learning blends cheap linear/RANS data with sparse LES/measurements to get the best of both.

9.3 ML for measurement & wakes

  • ML-MCP: gradient-boosted trees / neural nets ingest multiple reanalysis predictors and stability information to capture non-linear site–reference relationships, reducing long-term-correction uncertainty versus linear MCP (§4.2).
  • Wake surrogates: data-driven models trained on LES/SCADA reproduce wake deficits and interactions faster and sometimes more accurately than analytical wakes (Chapter 10), and are used inside layout/control optimisation.
  • Gap-filling & QC: learned models impute sensor gaps and flag anomalies in the ingestion stage (§3.3).

9.4 Foundation weather models

Large data-driven global forecast models (GraphCast, FourCastNet, Aurora and successors) and ML reanalyses are reshaping the reference-data landscape. Their relevance here is as next-generation, high-resolution, low-latency sources of the long-term climate and as priors for downscaling — a space the platform's data layer should be designed to plug into as these products mature.

Engineering discipline for MLData-driven engines must be bounded by physics and validation: always report a physics-based cross-check, quantify out-of-distribution risk (a surrogate trained on moderate terrain must not be trusted on a cliff), and version both model weights and training data for reproducibility and audit. ML is an accelerator and an uncertainty-reducer, not an excuse to skip the physics or the measurement.

Part III — Wakes, Energy & Optimisation

10. Wake Modelling & Blockage

A turbine extracts momentum, leaving a slower, more turbulent wake that reduces the energy of turbines behind it and adds to their fatigue loads. Wake losses are typically the single largest loss category (often 5–15%+), so wake modelling is central to both yield and layout. The platform ships a library of analytical wake models plus blockage, with the same pluggable interface as the flow engines.

10.1 The Jensen (Park) model

The original engineering wake: a top-hat (uniform) velocity deficit in a linearly expanding wake. Behind a turbine of rotor radius r0 and thrust coefficient CT, the wake radius and centreline velocity at downstream distance x are:

rw = r0 + k·x  ;   U(x) = U0 · [ 1 − (1 − √(1−CT)) / (1 + k·x/r0)² ]Eq. 10.1 — Jensen deficit

The single parameter is the wake-decay constant k (≈0.075 onshore, 0.04–0.05 offshore), often tied to ambient turbulence. Simple, fast, robust — still a sensible default and validation baseline, though it overestimates near-wake recovery on the centreline.

10.2 The Gaussian model (Bastankhah & Porté-Agel)

The modern default: a self-similar Gaussian deficit derived from mass and momentum conservation, far more physical in the near-to-mid wake. The normalised deficit at downstream distance x and radial offset r is:

ΔU/U0 = [ 1 − √(1 − CT / (8(σ/d0)²)) ] · exp( −r² / (2σ²) )Eq. 10.2 — Gaussian deficit
σ/d0 = k*·(x/d0) + ε,   ε ≈ 0.2√β,   β = ½(1+√(1−CT))/√(1−CT)Eq. 10.3 — wake width growth

The growth rate k* is linked to ambient turbulence intensity (Niayifar & Porté-Agel): k* ≈ 0.38·I0 + 0.004. This turbulence-coupled Gaussian model (often with the Niayifar–Porté-Agel superposition) is the recommended production wake model.

10.3 Other models the library must include

  • Frandsen: momentum-conserving model developed for regular arrays; pairs with the Frandsen turbulence model for loads.
  • Ainslie eddy-viscosity: solves the thin-shear-layer equations numerically; the engine behind DNV WindFarmer / openWind.
  • Larsen (EWTS II): a similarity-theory model used in WindPRO.
  • TurbOPark (Ørsted): Gaussian wake with turbulence-driven expansion that better captures deep-array and inter-farm effects; widely adopted 2020s for large/clustered farms.
  • Dynamic Wake Meandering (DWM): models the wake as a passively meandering deficit — physically rich, used for loads and control, more expensive.

10.4 Superposition & deep-array effects

With many turbines, individual wakes must be combined. The platform supports the standard schemes — linear sum, sum-of-squares (RSS), max-deficit, and momentum-conserving/energy-balance:

RSS:  (ΔUtotal/U0)² = Σi (ΔUi/U0)²Eq. 10.4 — sum-of-squares

For large arrays, the Frandsen infinite-array / deep-array correction (or TurbOPark) prevents under-prediction of losses in the interior of big wind farms where the boundary layer itself is modified.

10.5 Wake-added turbulence

Wakes raise downstream TI, which both speeds wake recovery and drives neighbour fatigue loads (Chapter 13). The engine adds wake turbulence via the Frandsen/IEC 61400-1 effective-turbulence formulation or the Crespo–Hernández relation:

Iadd = 0.73 · a0.83 · I00.03 · (x/d)−0.32Eq. 10.5 — Crespo–Hernández added TI

where a is the axial induction factor. Total TI at a turbine = ambient ⊕ wake-added (combined in quadrature), evaluated per upwind turbine and sector.

10.6 Blockage / global-induction effect active research

A wind farm slows the wind approaching it — the global blockage effect — reducing front-row energy and shifting the resource at the masts that measured it. Once neglected, it is now standard practice to account for: measured/modelled magnitudes are typically a ~1–2% energy reduction for large arrays, with observations of ~1–2% wind-speed reduction several rotor diameters upstream of offshore farms. Recent (2024–2025) work delivers engineering blockage parametrisations (TurbOPark-with-blockage, meso–micro perturbation models, induction/vortex models) suitable for inclusion alongside the wake engine. The platform models blockage as a self-induction field and reports it as an explicit line in the loss/energy budget, because it interacts with how the very masts that anchor the assessment are interpreted.

10.7 Validation

Engineering wake models are validated against SCADA and LES through community benchmarks — most notably FarmConners — which show that well-tuned Gaussian/turbulence-coupled models capture array losses within a few percent on average, with larger scatter case-by-case. The platform's wake module should be benchmark-tested against these datasets and report per-model agreement so the analyst can choose defensibly.

11. Energy Yield Assessment — Gross to Net

This is the chapter that produces the number the project lives or dies by. It converts the wind resource and layout into gross energy, then subtracts an itemised waterfall of losses to reach net energy — the P50 around which uncertainty (Chapter 12) is wrapped.

11.1 Gross energy

For each turbine, the predicted sector-wise Weibull climate (from the flow engine) is combined with the manufacturer power curve P(U), normalised to site air density (IEC 61400-12-1), and integrated:

AEPgross,turbine = 8760 · Σsectors fs · ∫ P(U) · fs(U) dUEq. 11.1 — gross AEP

summed over turbines for the farm gross. The integral runs over the Weibull distribution per sector; density correction shifts the power curve (or, equivalently, scales the wind speed) per IEC. Rotor-equivalent wind speed (accounting for shear/veer across the swept area) is the modern refinement over hub-height-only integration.

11.2 The loss waterfall

Gross energy is reduced by losses grouped into the categories standardised by IEC 61400-15 and industry practice (DNV, openWind). Figure 6 shows the waterfall; Table 11.1 gives the categories with typical onshore ranges the tool ships as editable defaults.

AEPnet = AEPgross · Πj (1 − Lj)  ;   CF = AEPnet / (Prated · 8760)Eq. 11.2 — net AEP & capacity factor

Losses combine multiplicatively (not additively) because each acts on the energy surviving the previous ones. The net AEP is the P50 central estimate; the capacity factor (typically 25–40% for good onshore Indian sites) is the headline efficiency metric. The tool reports per-turbine and farm-level net AEP, CF, full-load hours, and the complete waterfall — and exports it in the IEC 61400-15-2 EYA digital exchange format for downstream interoperability.

Why itemise losses this wayLenders and independent engineers expect this taxonomy. Implementing the IEC 61400-15 categories natively — each editable, each carrying its own uncertainty — makes the tool's output directly bankable and review-ready, rather than a black-box AEP number.

12. Uncertainty Quantification & P-Values

A P50 without an uncertainty band is not bankable. This chapter specifies how the platform assembles the uncertainty budget and converts it into the exceedance probabilities (P75, P90, P99) that size debt and price risk. This is where a rigorous tool separates itself from a spreadsheet.

12.1 The uncertainty budget

Total energy uncertainty is built from independent components, each estimated as a percentage standard deviation and combined in quadrature (assuming independence):

σtotal = √( σmeas² + σLT² + σvert² + σflow² + σwake² + σpower² + σloss² + σIAV² )Eq. 12.1 — combined uncertainty

12.2 From σ to P-values

Assuming an approximately normal energy distribution, exceedance levels are the P50 minus a multiple of σ:

PX = P50 · ( 1 − zX · σtotal )  ;   z75=0.674,  z90=1.282,  z99=2.326Eq. 12.2 — exceedance levels

So with σtotal=8%, P90 ≈ P50 × (1 − 1.282×0.08) ≈ 0.90 × P50. A lognormal assumption is sometimes preferred to keep energy positive and skew realistic; the platform should offer both.

12.3 Assessment horizon — why P9010yr > P901yr

Inter-annual variability is the only component that averages out over time: over an N-year horizon its contribution divides by √N. The platform therefore reports P-values for the financially-relevant horizons (1-year for operational checks; 10-year and 20-year for debt sizing), recomputing σtotal with the horizon-scaled IAV term. This single feature materially affects financeability and is often mishandled in ad-hoc spreadsheets.

The commercial case for accuracyEvery percentage point shaved off σtotal lifts P90 and enlarges the debt the project can raise. The investment in better flow modelling (CFD/ML), measurement (LiDAR + tall mast) and rigorous MCP is justified precisely here — uncertainty reduction is the product's economic value proposition.

13. Site Suitability & Turbine-Load Validation

Micrositing is not only about energy — each turbine position must be safe for the chosen turbine over its design life. The platform validates every position against the site-suitability requirements of IEC 61400-1 Ed.4 (2019, with the 2025/2026 amendment), the same checks WindPRO SITE COMPLIANCE and DNV WindFarmer perform.

13.1 Turbine classes

IEC 61400-1 defines wind classes by reference wind speed and turbulence category; a turbine is suitable only if every site parameter sits within its class envelope.

  • Extreme wind: 50-year extreme gust Ve50 vs class Vref (Ve50 ≈ 1.4 Vref).

13.3 Output & the optimisation link

For every candidate position the platform returns a pass/fail per parameter and an overall suitability verdict for each turbine model in the catalogue. Crucially, these checks are constraints in the layout optimiser (Chapter 14): a position that maximises energy but fails effective-turbulence (too close behind another turbine) is rejected or penalised. Where loads are borderline the tool flags the need for site-specific aeroelastic simulation (hand-off to OpenFAST-class load tools), rather than overstating its own remit.

Energy ↔ loads tensionPacking turbines tightly raises energy density but increases wake-added turbulence on downwind machines — the core trade-off micrositing must resolve. Suitability checking is therefore not a post-hoc gate but an objective-shaping constraint wired into the optimiser.

14. Layout Optimisation (Micrositing)

This is the act of micrositing itself: choosing turbine positions (and sometimes models and hub heights) to optimise an objective under constraints. The platform wraps the flow, wake, energy and suitability modules in an optimisation loop — the capability that turns an assessment tool into a design tool.

14.1 Objective functions

  • Maximise net AEP (or AEP per turbine) — the simplest, wake-dominated objective.
  • Minimise LCOE / maximise NPV or IRR — economically correct; trades energy against cabling, roads, foundations and O&M (TopFarm-style).
  • Minimise wake loss or maximise a robust (P90) yield — risk-aware objectives.

Beyond positions, energy can be raised by wake steering — yawing upstream turbines to deflect their wakes off downstream machines. Co-optimising layout and yaw control (FLORIS, PyWake) is an active frontier with demonstrated field gains; the platform should expose yaw as an optional design/operation variable and report the steering uplift.

14.5 Tools to build on

  • DTU PyWake (≈v2.6): fast vectorised engineering wake models + AEP — the evaluation core.
  • DTU TopFarm (≈v2.6, on OpenMDAO): layout/cost optimisation with IRR/NPV objectives and constraints.
  • NREL FLORIS: steady-state wake + control (wake steering) optimisation.
  • FOXES (Fraunhofer): scalable wake/farm modelling; OpenMDAO/pyOptSparse: the optimisation backbone.

The recommendation: do not rebuild the wake/optimisation maths — wrap PyWake + TopFarm/OpenMDAO as the optimisation engine and contribute the platform's terrain-aware flow fields and constraint set, which is where the incumbents are weak.

Part IV — Building the Tool

15. Software Architecture

The science of Parts I–III becomes software here. The architecture is a layered pipeline with a single project data model and pluggable engines, so flow models, wake models and optimisers can be swapped without touching the rest of the system.

15.1 Layered architecture

15.2 The project data model — the keystone

One serialisable Project object ties everything together: site geometry, measurement campaigns + QC'd time series, the derived wind climate(s), terrain/roughness/obstacle layers, the turbine catalogue, scenarios (layouts), and results (WRG, AEP, losses, P-values, suitability). Designing this schema well — versioned, with full provenance — is the highest-leverage early decision; every engine reads and writes it, and interoperability with WAsP (.lib, .wrg, .tab) and the IEC EYA exchange format hangs off it.

15.3 The engine interface

Each flow engine implements the same contract — predict(climate, terrain, points) → WRG — so the orchestration layer treats linear, CFD, mesoscale and ML engines identically and can ensemble them or A/B them on one site. Wake models and optimisers follow the same discipline. This is the architectural expression of the "full-spectrum" thesis: fidelity becomes a runtime choice, not a rebuild.

15.4 Compute & orchestration

Linear and analytical-wake runs are interactive (seconds). CFD and mesoscale are batch (a job queue + workers, optionally cloud/HPC or GPU). The orchestration layer abstracts this so the UI experience is consistent: submit a scenario, track the job, retrieve results. Caching of expensive flow fields keyed by terrain+inflow lets the optimiser reuse solves.
